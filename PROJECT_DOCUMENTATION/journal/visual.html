🔍 Phase 1 — Observation

The Nature of the Data

Understanding the raw material before any model is built. What does fraud actually look like in 284,807 transactions?

We begin with a dataset of 284,807 European credit card transactions collected over two days. The challenge is immediately visible: fraud constitutes merely 0.1727% of all traffic — an imbalance ratio of 1:577. A naive system that approves everything achieves 99.83% accuracy while catching zero fraud.

Key Observation

Fraudulent transactions have a lower median amount ($9.25 vs $22.00) but higher mean ($122.21 vs $88.29), suggesting a bimodal pattern: small test charges followed by high-value exploitation.

We also investigated temporal patterns. By extracting a cyclic hour-of-day feature, a stark pattern emerges: fraud rate peaks at 1.71% around 2 AM, compared to below 0.05% during busy midday hours. Fraudsters exploit low-monitoring windows.

⚡ Phase 2 — Revelation

Why Simple Models Fail

The first attempt reveals a fundamental tension: precision vs recall in imbalanced landscapes.

Our first model, a Logistic Regression with balanced class weights, achieves a respectable ROC-AUC of 0.9712 and catches 89.8% of fraud. But there's a devastating catch: precision is merely 5.35%. For every real fraud caught, the system falsely blocks 17.7 innocent customers.

The Revelation

A single threshold on a linear model cannot simultaneously achieve high precision and high recall in extreme imbalance. We need a non-linear model AND a smarter decision framework.

🔧 Phase 3 — Approach

💡 Phase 5 — Explainability

Opening the Black Box with SHAP

Every decision must be explainable. SHAP deconstructs each prediction into per-feature contributions.

Regulatory frameworks demand that fraud decisions be interpretable. Using TreeSHAP, we decompose every prediction into per-feature contributions. Globally, V14 (mean |SHAP| = 1.540), V4 (1.410), and V10 (0.646) are the top fraud discriminators. Notably, our engineered hour feature ranked 15th (0.183), validating temporal engineering.

Fig 16. SHAP beeswarm plot — each dot is a test sample; position is SHAP value; color is feature value.
Fig 17. Mean |SHAP| importance bar chart — V14 dominates decisively.

Individual force plots reveal why each transaction was flagged. This is especially powerful for the ABSTAIN zone — reviewers can see exactly which features created model disagreement:

🛡 Phase 6 — Improvement

Defense in Depth: Novelty Detection & Calibration

Two final layers that transform a good system into a production-grade one.

Isolation Forest Novelty Layer

Supervised models only know patterns from their training data. An entirely new fraud vector — never seen before — will bypass the XGBoost ensemble. To defend against this, we train an Isolation Forest exclusively on 227,451 legitimate transactions. It learns the "shape" of normal behavior and flags anything geometrically distant.

Defense in Depth

By integrating the IF with the 2D engine, any anomalous AUTO_APPROVE is upgraded to STEP_UP_AUTH, and STEP_UP is upgraded to MANUAL_REVIEW. This rescued 1 stealth fraud case that all 5 XGBoost models missed.

🎯 Conclusion

Uncertainty Is Not a Bug — It's a Feature

The MARI framework proves that epistemic uncertainty — conventionally treated as statistical noise — is a critical safety signal. By elevating uncertainty to a first-class citizen of the decision pipeline, we capture edge cases, defer to humans when appropriate, and produce a system that knows what it doesn't know, achieving an ultimate ROC-AUC of 0.9852 at $1.14/transaction.
